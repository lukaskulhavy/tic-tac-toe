What Drives Best-in-Class CIR
Revenue growth outpaces cost growth
McKinsey: 68% of CIR outperformance is a revenue story, not cost-cutting. Market leadership, fee income diversification, and pricing power expand the denominator faster than the numerator grows.
Nordic banks, CIBC: fee income diversification and market leadership sustain wide revenue base
No branch / legacy infrastructure overhead
Neobanks avoid the single largest fixed-cost line in traditional banking. Revolut's support costs rose only 5% despite 38% customer growth — a structural advantage impossible to replicate by optimising a branch network.
Revolut, Starling: zero property footprint, automated onboarding
Concentrated markets + oligopoly pricing
Nordic banking markets are dominated by 2–4 players. High market share reduces cost of customer acquisition and supports margin — the efficiency ratio benefits from both sides simultaneously.
Handelsbanken, DNB, Nordea: ~42% sector avg vs. 50%+ in fragmented EU markets
AI replacing process steps — not just augmenting
Leading institutions are automating full decision workflows (credit, fraud, AML) — not just assisting analysts. The CIR benefit only materialises when headcount models are redesigned, not when AI is layered on top of existing teams.
TD: mortgage approval hours → minutes; CIBC: 600k hrs saved
Low-cost personnel base (structural)
Some efficiency leaders benefit from structurally lower salary costs. This advantage is geographic and not replicable through management practice alone — an important caveat when benchmarking against emerging market peers.
Nordic banks benefit from lean governance models; neobanks from remote-first, global talent pools
Key takeaway for COOs
Best-in-class CIR is rarely achieved by cutting costs alone. The winning formula is: grow revenue faster than costs, automate process steps (not just support), and structurally reduce fixed-cost commitments (branches, legacy platforms). Cost reduction without revenue growth typically stalls at ~55%.
